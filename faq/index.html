Frequently Asked Questions

What specific policies would you like to see changed?

We would like relevant offices in the government to do the following:

  • Adopt an emergency regulation, executive order, or FDA/CMS waiver that would allow the production, sale and distribution of paper-strip screening tests to check if people are likely contagious with COVID-19.

  • Allow people to take these public health screening tests without a prescription.

  • Clarify that those who take the test should not have to report the results.

  • Inform consumers that the screening tests may not be used for a diagnosis.

  • Use appropriations to buy/subsidize and distribute these tests as soon as possible.

What are rapid paper-strip tests and how do they differ from other tests?

Rapid paper-strip tests make it easy to test for SAS-CoV-2 contagiousness, with immediate results. If everyone could be tested frequently and stayed home when they were contagious, we might be able to reopen schools and offices.

Rapid paper-strip tests are one of three types of tests for SARS-CoV-2 infections.

Simple rapid paper-strip tests. These tests can be mass produced at tens of millions per day and can therefore be taken frequently, which research shows is critical for screening. They could be taken at home and do not require an electronic device. Each test is a simple paper strip. To make them easy to use, people could either insert them into a reusable plastic cassette, or they could use a mobile phone app to read the results.

Encased rapid paper-strip tests. Some rapid paper-strip tests, like the Abbott BinaxNOW, come with a special cardboard or plastic cassette, which helps with usability. Because it takes time for factories to assemble the cassette with the strip, factories can only produce 1 million per day, which is useful but not enough for widespread daily testing. We believe the better approach is to use simple rapid paper strip tests, which can be paired with a reusable plastic cassette or mobile phone app for better usability.

Rapid tests that require an electronic device. These tests deliver results quickly, but they require a separate electronic device. Although they are more sensitive than paper-strip tests, their manufacturing complexity makes it less likely that they can be produced in sufficient quantity for widespread frequent use. Example manufacturers include Quidel, Becton Dickinson, Orasure, Mammoth, and LumiraDx.

Standard PCR. This type of test is hypersensitive and can detect the virus for days or weeks after you have stopped being contagious. PCR tests must typically be evaluated in an advanced laboratory setting. Because of their cost and operational complexity, it is very unlikely that PCR tests could be performed frequently.

Each type of test is useful in its own way, but right now it is illegal to sell paper-strip tests without a prescription. 

We do not have a financial interest in any of these tests. We just want this information to be available to policy makers.

Are rapid tests available right now?

The technology to make paper-strip tests exists, but has up until very recently not received regulatory approval. On August 26, the FDA approved the Abbott BinaxNOW, a rapid test that relatively inexpensive and easy to use. While a step in the right direction, these tests still require a prescription and are much slower to produce than simple paper strip tests.

Rapid tests that require electronic devices are also heavily regulated and in most cases legally require evaluation in a central laboratory, even though the technology allows for evaluation in schools or offices.

Why aren't rapid tests widely available?

We don’t have these tests yet because they do not meet overly strict sensitivity requirements.  

Regulators have required paper-strip tests to be nearly as sensitive as diagnostic tests, which means they need to be able to detect trace amounts of viral RNA that exist even after the person has stopped being contagious. Diagnostic tests for SARS-Cov-2 are currently performed using a method called PCR, which can detect extremely small amounts of virus.

The broader problem is that public health screening tools do not have their own regulatory path.  Since they give individuals results, they're currently classified as 'diagnostic', a path burdened with requirements around prescriptions, lab supervision, and reporting (on top of the inappropriate sensitivity requirement).

Why should we test daily (or every other day)?  

A person can go from not being contagious to being very contagious in about 2 days. If you only test weekly, then you could easily be contagious for 5-6 days without knowing it, and be spreading the virus.  If you test daily, you are much likelier to know the first day you are contagious. 

Why do we need tests that can be done at home?  

To stop community spread, we have to stop everyone from spreading the virus. We support testing at schools and the office. But since not everyone can get tested that way, we want people to be able to test themselves at home.

What is test sensitivity? 

There are two types of sensitivity. 

  • If someone has the virus (or remnants of the virus) in their system, diagnostic sensitivity is the probability that a test will detect it.

  • If someone is contagious with the virus, contagiousness sensitivity is the probability that a test will detect the virus.

If you want to diagnose or treat someone, a test like PCR with high diagnostic sensitivity is needed. This type of test can detect trace amounts of viral RNA debris that remain in your system for weeks after you have stopped being contagious. In medical settings, this type of sensitivity is important.

If you want to know if someone is contagious (for public health screening), contagiousness sensitivity is what matters. Rapid tests have excellent contagiousness sensitivity, comparable to PCR.

Will rapid paper-strip tests give people a false sense of security?

Rapid paper-strip tests are like masks. They don’t provide guaranteed security but they reduce the spread and allow you to do things you couldn’t otherwise. People still need to be cautious when wearing a mask. We think the same will happen with rapid tests.

What about false positives?

A false positive is when a test incorrectly reports detecting a condition that isn’t there.  Most rapid tests have low false-positive rates. The impact of false positives can be further reduced by performing repeat tests or follow-up diagnostic tests. 

That said, false positives are undesirable and may keep some people away from work. That is a relatively small down side compared to the current state, in which many contagious people aren’t tested at all and by default behave as false negatives! Rapid paper-strip tests will reduce the number of people who are contagious but don’t know it, giving people more freedom overall.

See also this video from Michael Mina, explaining how the impact of false positive can be greatly reduced with repeat tests that check for different parts of the virus.

Is one type of test best?

There is no ‘best’ test.  Each type has a role.

  • To make a clinical diagnosis or provide data for scientific studies evaluating viral mechanisms:  Standard PCR  

  • Where groups congregate daily and machines can be purchased or rented:  Rapid tests that require an electronic device.

  • For all other uses and settings: Daily rapid paper-strip tests

Where should rapid paper-strip tests roll out first?  

The tests could first be deployed in schools and nursing homes. As more tests become available, workplaces could have priority. Then, other gathering places could be added, like colleges, sporting events, public transport, and restaurants.

They should also be sent to hot spots to get the epidemic under control.

Ideally everyone should take the test before leaving home, and every American would have access to a 20-pack every month. 

Can we get rid of masks and social distancing once we have paper-strip tests?

While this is certainly a mid-term aim of such a testing strategy, we should not expect to be able to do this at the very beginning. Since not everyone will be able or willing to take rapid tests, we should initially view this as an addition to our toolbox, not as a silver bullet replacing everything else. We hope that rapid contagiousness tests, if used sufficiently frequently by everyone, would push down the prevalence of COVID-19 sufficiently to make other measures unnecessary.

Where can I find rapid tests?

Rapid tests are currently very difficult to obtain, but a good resource is the University of Arizona Testing Commons. It allows you to explore different options with various regulatory statuses and required analysis locations. If you want truly rapid tests, you will need to filter the analysis location to “CLIA Waived / POC” and “Self”.

How did you determine the viral load threshold for infectiousness?

Several papers have found that active (“live”) virus can be cultured only when viral load exceeds 10^5 or 10^6 cp/ml. The precise threshold is not critical. What matters is that rapid testing removes most of those who are contagious from workplaces, schools and shops. Sources: Quicke et. al. Wolfel et. al. Cevik et. al.

Is $1 a realistic price for these tests?

Yes. Paper strip tests for malaria have been known to wholesale for as low as $0.20 for large purchase orders over 100 million tests. [Source: The Global Fund. Select “Diagnostic Tests” and then “Malaria RDT: P.f.”]

Can we afford to pay for frequent, widespread testing?

A testing program will pay for itself hundred of times over.

Consider a K-8 testing program. If each student of 35 million K-8 students were to take a $3 test twice a week for 40 weeks, the cost would be $8 Billion. Not only would such a program provide significant emotional and educational benefits to American parents and students, it would more than pay for itself. According to the Brookings Institute, the cost to future earnings of four months of lost education is $2.5 trillion, which could be salvaged by a rapid testing program. There are also immediate advantages to the economic output of the parents. If we assume that parents contribute 15% of US economic output and if we conservatively assume that homebound children reduce the output of their parents by 10%, a program that brings kids back to school will preserve $300 Billion in GDP. In short, a K-8 testing program could provide a 40X return on investment in the current year and a 300X return on investment in future years. Even larger benefits will be obtained by extending these testing programs to essential businesses.

What can I do to help promote daily rapid paper-strip tests?

  1. If you haven’t already, write your representatives and governor.  If you really want to get through and be heard or talk details, ask for email addresses of health staffers. They may be open to talking, and are among the people who decide which ideas deserve the official's time.

  2. Contact anyone else you know in elected office. Elected officials want to know about good ideas they can propose to constituents. You don’t need to be an expert. Click here for example language.

  3. Write letters to the editor of your local paper. See here for a good example. Mentioning rapidtests.org is a good way to drive more letters to policy makers.

  4. Talk to your friends. When they say, ‘what’s new with you’, tell them about daily quick tests. (“I just learned that if everyone could test themselves daily for COVID-19 we would get the pandemic under control fast. The technology even exists to do it!”)

  5. If you belong to any workers unions or teachers unions, ask them to demand rapid tests.

  6. Spread the word about daily paper-strip testing on twitter. @mention people who have influence, even if their following is not huge.

  7. @mention reporters who cover COVID-19 or testing. If you read an article, send a tweet.  It’s okay if the reporter is from a small news outlet -- the goal is to spread the message, and reporters from small outlets yearn to break important news. 

  8. Share links to the rapidtests.org website, FB group, and videos (see below).

  9. Share videos on social media. Make your own video and tell us about it!  Start a video contest.
